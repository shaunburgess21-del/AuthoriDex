Yeah — that concern is real, and it’s one of those “trust-killer” moments for a first-time user:

“368,443… okay… is that dollars? followers? votes? why is it so big? can I trust it?”

The good news: you don’t need to reveal your whole formula (and you shouldn’t, because of the jackpot / gaming). You just need to anchor meaning and show provenance in a way that feels intuitive.

What the Trend Score needs to communicate (without exposing the recipe)

Users don’t actually need the full math. They need 4 things:

What it is: “An attention index (points), not followers, not money.”

What it compares: “Comparable across people. Higher = more attention right now.”

Where it comes from: “Search + News + Wikipedia (updated hourly).”

Why it moved: “Driven by X/Y/Z (Momentum Signals + Score Drivers).”

If you nail those, the big number stops feeling arbitrary.

Keep big numbers (for jackpot), but make them feel interpretable

A 0–100 score is intuitive, but you’re right: it makes jackpot guessing easier.

A better compromise is exactly what you suggested: a wider “index points” range (e.g., 1,000–50,000 or 1,000–500,000) plus extra context that gives meaning without giving predictability.

The trick: show meaning next to the number, not instead of it

On the leaderboard row and on the person page, add one small line like:

“#1 Today • Top 0.3% • Updated 19m ago”

“Attention Index (points) • Based on Search, News, Wikipedia”

Now the number is “just a unit”, and the rank + percentile carry the intuitive meaning.

Percentile is huge here because it answers the user’s real question:

“Is this big or small?”

The best “What is this score?” explanation (simple, non-gameable)

Add an ⓘ tooltip / modal next to “Trend Score” with wording like:

Trend Score = Attention Index

We track how much attention someone is getting from:

Search interest

News coverage

Wikipedia curiosity

We normalize these signals and combine them into a single index score.

The score is designed to be comparable across people, and updates hourly.

What matters most is rank + change, not the absolute number.

Then include a tiny “Sources” footer and a “Last updated” timestamp (you already do this nicely).

This explains first principles without telling anyone how to reverse-engineer the jackpot.

About your “why is it always in this range?” point (your instinct is right)

You should ask Replit for the “first principles” of your numeric range. Specifically:

What’s the final scaling constant / mapping that turns the internal model into ~200k–400k?

Is it tied to:

distribution of the top 100?

a baseline anchor person?

a normalization target (mean/median)?

Does the range drift as your dataset grows?

Once you understand that, you can confidently message users with:

“Scores typically range between X and Y”

“A +10% move is meaningful”

Even better: you can lock the range with a stable mapping (percentile/log-based) while keeping unpredictability.

Recommended approach for the new scale (keeps jackpot hard)

If you do decide to move from hundreds of thousands:

Best option: Percentile → Points mapping

Compute each person’s percentile each run (or daily).

Map percentile to a points range (e.g., 1,000–50,000) using a curve (log-ish).

Display integer points.

This keeps:

interpretability (bounded range)

comparability

anti-gaming (still hard to nail an exact value)

And it avoids “why is this 368,443?” because users already understand bounded “index points”.
