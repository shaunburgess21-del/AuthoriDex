Claude’s take is very good, and I agree with the prioritization shift:

1) Backfill + staleness alerts are the best “pre-launch reliability ROI”

You’ve already done the big stability work (timeouts, retries, stale lock cleanup, zeroGuard). Backfill is the next step that turns “sometimes late” into “almost always complete.”

Why backfill matters: users don’t care why an hour was missed—only that charts/24h deltas feel continuous. Backfilling 1–3 missed slots after the next successful run gets you most of the way to “feels reliable” without needing perfect uptime.

Minimal backfill strategy (safe + simple):

Detect missing hourly buckets between last_successful_hour and current_hour.

Backfill up to N (e.g., 3) oldest missing buckets per run.

Mark backfilled runs clearly in ingestion_runs (e.g., isBackfill=true, targetHour=...) so you can observe it.

Keep the same locking to avoid overlap.

Alerts are equally simple and huge value: “latest snapshot age > 2–3h” → ping.

2) I mostly agree with “don’t queue yet”

I’m with Claude here: “job queue semantics” is nice, but right now it’s not what moves the needle most. You already addressed the failure modes that were actually biting you (Serper hangs, stuck runs, visibility gaps).

If you do anything “queue-ish” now, make it tiny:

Idempotency key = (score_version, hour_bucket) so backfills can’t duplicate.

Keep the lock and cron trigger.

That’s enough.

3) The scaling/normalization point is the real future landmine

This is the one I’d add to your “things to plan before adding 200+ people.”

If your per-source “0–100” velocity sub-scores are cohort percentiles, then expanding the cohort changes the mapping for everyone even if their raw signals don’t change. That can create a system-wide re-ranking wave that looks like a bug (because it feels like “everyone moved for no reason”).

How to think about it

You currently have two separate “reference frames”:

Time-based stability: EMA smoothing reduces hour-to-hour noise.

Population-based stability: normalization defines what “high” means relative to others.

EMA helps the first. It does not protect you from the second.

Options (pick the one that matches your product philosophy)

Option A — Fixed reference distribution (recommended for “trust + long-term consistency”)

Choose a calibration set (e.g., your initial 100, or a curated “anchor” set).

Compute normalization parameters from that anchor set:

For percentiles: store a reference CDF / quantile cutpoints.

For z-score style: store mean/std, or robust stats (median/MAD).

New people are scored against the same reference.
Result: adding people doesn’t reshuffle the universe.

Option B — Rolling cohort percentiles (fine if you accept “relative leaderboard”)

Keep cohort percentiles, but then you need guardrails:

Introduce new people gradually (staging pool → full cohort).

Version the normalization (normalization_v3) and roll it out intentionally.

Expect a “recalibration event” when you add big batches; message it in UI or smooth the transition over days.

Option C — Hybrid anchor + cohort

Use cohort percentiles within category (Politics vs Sports) while anchoring overall scale.

Or blend: score = 0.7 * anchor_norm + 0.3 * cohort_percentile
This reduces cohort shock while still adapting.

The cheapest “now” step that prevents pain later

Before you expand beyond 100:

Decide whether the score is meant to be stable in an absolute sense over months, or purely relative to “who we’re tracking.”

If you want stability: implement an anchor reference for normalization and treat changes as a versioned event.
