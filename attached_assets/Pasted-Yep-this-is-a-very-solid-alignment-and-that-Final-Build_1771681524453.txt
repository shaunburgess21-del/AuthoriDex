1) Why “Score Drivers” is interesting (to normal users)

It’s interesting because it answers the only question people really have when they see a number move:

“Why did this person jump (or drop)?”

Without drivers, your score can feel like a black box. With drivers, it becomes a story:

News-heavy (e.g., 70% News): “They’re in the headlines right now.”

Search-heavy (e.g., 70% Search): “People are suddenly curious—something sparked attention.”

Wiki-heavy (e.g., 50% Wiki): “People are researching them (curiosity spike), even if news isn’t huge.”

That creates:

trust (it’s not random)

shareability (“look, this move was 80% search-driven”)

differentiation (you’re not just ranking—you’re explaining)

Also: drivers are not your fixed weights (40/35/25). They’re the attribution of what changed over the time window. So it won’t always look like “40/30/30.” It’ll often be lopsided when something real happens (e.g., 85% News after a scandal, or 90% Search after a viral moment).

2) Will it be fresh for the bottom half of the leaderboard?

It’ll be fresh in the sense that it updates on the same schedule, but it may be less “interesting” because low-trending people often have small or flat signals.

What you should expect for the bottom half:

Smaller deltas → drivers might show “No strong driver” (which is fine and honest)

More zeros/low counts especially for news → but your caching + widening + holds should prevent obvious weirdness

Still updated hourly (with news refreshing every 2 hours), but movement is naturally slower

How to keep it useful for low-trend people:

Show drivers only when movement crosses a threshold, e.g.:

display Score Drivers if abs(change_24h) >= 1.5% or if sum(abs(contributions)) exceeds a minimum

otherwise show: “Stable (no major signal shift)”

Or keep it collapsed behind “Why did this change?” so it doesn’t clutter.

So yes: fresh, but often quiet—and that’s actually a good signal in itself.
