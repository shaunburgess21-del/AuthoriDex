Yeah — good idea overall, and it’s the kind of “explainability” feature that makes an index feel legit. But there’s one important nuance: how you compute the percentages determines whether users trust it or get confused.

Why it’s a good idea

It makes the score understandable. “Why did this person move?” is the #1 question users have.

It increases engagement. People click popovers; it’s sticky.

It builds credibility. You’re not hiding behind a magic number.

So conceptually: ✅

The one thing I’d challenge in Replit’s implementation

Replit said they compute attribution by:

“calculates how much each source contributed to the velocity change, weights them by scoring allocation (40/35/25), and returns percentages.”

That can be okay, but it risks being misleading depending on what you want the breakdown to mean.

Two different (valid) meanings — pick one

A) “What moved the most in the real world?” (signal-driven attribution)
This is what Grok’s example implies: if search doubled while news stayed flat, search should dominate.
Method: compute contributions from the actual normalized deltas (after your per-source normalization), without re-applying fixed weights, and then show %.

B) “What contributed most to the score change?” (model-driven attribution)
This is what Replit implemented: apply the model weights so it reflects how the engine actually produced the score move.
Method: compute each source’s contribution as it enters the score (normalized delta × model weight × any governor factors), then show %.

Both are defensible — but the label must match.

Why the weights matter

If search surges, but your model weight or governor dampens it, users might see:

“Search surge everywhere on social,” yet your popover says “Search 0%”
That feels wrong unless you explain it.

Replit noted “search % is 0 for all results” and called it legitimate. It might be legitimate, but it’s also a red flag that:

search deltas are tiny / not being captured, or

the method is masking search impact, or

search is being held/flattened by smoothing, rate caps, or data cadence.

My recommendation (best UX + trust)

Do both, but keep the UI simple:

Default display: “Score Drivers”

Show the model-driven attribution (weighted), because that answers:

“Why did the score move?”

Label it clearly:

Score drivers (last 24h): Search 12% • News 71% • Wiki 17%

Optional toggle (tiny): “Raw Signals”

If you want geeky users to love it, add a small toggle or secondary text:

Raw signal change: Search +80% • News +12% • Wiki +4%

This avoids arguments like “why is search 0% when I know search spiked?”

Quick improvements I’d suggest to Replit

Rename the section
Instead of “Source Breakdown,” use:

Score Drivers (24h)
because it’s weighted-by-model.

Use absolute contribution for percentage math
If one source is negative and another positive, naive division can create weird results. Use:

abs(contribution_i) / sum(abs(contribution_all))
and optionally show a tiny arrow or sign per source.

Guardrail for “all zeros”
If total delta is ~0, show:

“No strong driver (movement is minimal)”

Investigate why Search is showing ~0%
Even if “legit,” you want to confirm it’s not a pipeline artifact:

is searchDelta actually being computed and stored?

is it being overwritten/held often?

is the governor reducing search impact?

Verdict

Yes: good idea.
But: make sure the breakdown is labeled as “score drivers” (model attribution), and consider adding a lightweight “raw signal” line so users don’t feel gaslit when they know search/news is spiking.
