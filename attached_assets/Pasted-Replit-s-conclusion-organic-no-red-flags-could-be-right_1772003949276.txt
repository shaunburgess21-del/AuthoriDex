Replit’s conclusion (“organic, no red flags”) could be right — but the way it justified it has a couple of weak spots, and there are 2–3 very specific sanity checks worth doing before you fully trust it.

First: things in Replit’s answer that are a bit shaky
1) The “FBI Director confirmation” explanation is a narrative leap

It might be true, but Replit didn’t prove causality — it’s guessing a reason. The data (news/search/wiki moving) is what matters.

If you want a clean, shareable diagnostic, ask it to show:

top news headlines + sources for Kash in that window (not a story)

raw evidence that those articles are about the right person (entity disambiguation)

2) “Search volume jumped from 64th to 74th percentile” — watch that wording

If “search volume” is a percentile-like score, fine. If it’s an absolute volume metric, that phrasing is suspicious. Make sure you’re comparing apples to apples:

raw search_volume (whatever unit you store)

vs search_velocity_score (0–100 normalized)

3) “Wikipedia views ~80k” is huge — confirm it isn’t a mapping bug

80k daily pageviews can happen, but if that’s suddenly appearing, it’s worth verifying:

correct Wikipedia page being tracked (not another Patel)

no “pageviews API fallback returned nonsense”

no stale pageviews from the wrong date being reused

Second: the volatility itself doesn’t automatically mean “broken”

If your engine is doing what you designed (velocity-heavy + multi-signal), then:

one person spiking across news + search + wiki simultaneously

while another cools off in news

…can absolutely reshuffle #1–#5 within hours.

But you want to check whether the jump was within expected guardrails (rate caps, alpha tiers, no artifacts).
