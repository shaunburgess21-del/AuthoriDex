Perfect — that spec is detailed enough to sanity-check the actual mechanics, the cap question, and the UX implications.

1) Is there a “hard cap”?

Yes: Step 6 clamps Fame Score to 0–1,000,000:

fameIndex = clamp(round(baseScore * diversityMultiplier * 10000), 0, 1000000)

So displayed Fame Score can’t exceed 1,000,000, even if the blended baseScore would imply it.

Should you keep the cap?

For your current product positioning (relative “who’s hot now” across a fixed population of ~100), keeping a cap is actually good UX:

Users learn what numbers mean (e.g., 450k vs 650k).

Visual design stays stable.

Prevents “number inflation” over months/years.

If you ever expand to thousands of people and want the number to represent more absolute “global attention,” you can revisit, but right now the cap helps you.

2) Bigger issue than the cap: your mapping can unintentionally compress the top end

Right now:

baseScore is 0–100

You multiply by 10,000 → 0–1,000,000

So 609,000 implies baseScore ≈ 60.9 (assuming diversity=1.0).

That means your “top of leaderboard” living around 500k–700k is totally plausible — but whether you ever see 900k–1,000k depends on whether baseScore can realistically reach 90–100 given your normalization + damping.

The real question to answer (for UX):

Do you want 1,000,000 to be:

A mythical ceiling (almost never reached except global mega-events)? ✅ good for drama

Or a reachable top (top people sometimes hit it)? ❌ often makes rankings feel “maxed out”

I’d lean mythical ceiling. It makes “approaching 1M” feel like a rare moment.

3) A couple of things in this formula that are worth flagging
A) Mass score annualization is a bit odd

You take wikiPageviews7dAvg * 365, then log-scale between 10,000 annual and 1B annual.

That means mass is essentially:

“Daily avg pageviews, stretched to annual”

Log10-scaled into 0–100

It’s fine, but the choice of (10k → 0, 1B → 100) matters a lot. If your population grows beyond 100 celebs, you might want to derive the min/max from percentiles instead of fixed constants so it remains calibrated.

Simple improvement: clamp mass mapping based on population stats (p5 to p95) rather than fixed (1e4, 1e9) targets.

B) Velocity normalization is relative to your tracked set (not the world)

You’re doing percentile ranking vs the 100 tracked celebrities. That’s totally fine for an MVP, but it implies:

If you swap in/out people, the scale shifts.

If you expand to 500–5,000 people, velocity distribution changes and old scores aren’t comparable.

UX implication: The score is an “index within this set.” That’s okay — just be aware.

If you want the score to feel stable over time, eventually you’ll want:

a fixed “reference population” distribution, or

population stats computed over a larger stable universe.

C) Anti-spam damping is strong (and probably good)

velocityAdjusted = velocityScore * (0.35 + 0.65*(mass/100))

So a person with mass=0 only gets 35% of velocity. That’s a good anti-gaming rule.

But it also means: newcomers / niche people are structurally suppressed, even if they genuinely trend.

A nicer UX version (later) is:

keep damping, but allow “breakout exceptions” when 2–3 sources corroborate (which you already track via spikingSourceCount).

D) Your “Confidence Score” currently penalizes missing X handle

You said: “legacy, currently applies to all since X is disabled.”

If that penalty is actually used anywhere meaningful, it will mislead (everyone gets punished equally). If it’s only displayed internally, ignore. If it touches ranking/scoring, remove/neutralize it until X is live.
