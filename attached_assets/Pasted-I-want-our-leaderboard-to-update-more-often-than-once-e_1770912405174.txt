Yes — you can make it “tick along” every 5–10 minutes without blowing up cost or trust, as long as you structure it as a two-speed pipeline (fast lane + slow lane) and you’re honest about what’s “live” vs “fully refreshed”.

Here’s the clean way to do it.

The two-speed model (works smoothly)
1) Slow lane (authoritative) — every 60 min

This is your current full ingestion:

Wikipedia pageviews

GDELT headlines/mentions

Serper search

Recompute full fame score, write snapshots, update ranks.

This remains the source of truth.

2) Fast lane (cosmetic + lightweight signal) — every 5–10 min

Between full ingests, you run a much smaller job that:

updates only the “velocity” portion of the score (small weight)

or updates a “live momentum” overlay used for ordering/animation but not the core score

uses only the source(s) that can handle frequent calls + your cached data

This gives the site that “Bloomberg terminal” feel, without pretending you have full fresh data every 10 minutes.

Which of your 3 APIs should be the fast lane?
Best candidate: Wikipedia pageviews

It’s robust and free-ish (public API).

You can hit it more often without cost anxiety.

Important gotcha: Wikipedia pageviews data is usually not real-time; it updates on an hourly/daily cadence depending on endpoint. So polling every 10 minutes might often return the same value.

Still useful if you treat it as a stabilizing background signal and only let it nudge things.

GDELT

Can be spiky, rate-limited, and sometimes noisy.

Frequent polling increases false positives (“wrong person” issues) unless your disambiguation is strong.

I’d keep it hourly or maybe every 30 min if you have strong caching + query hygiene.

Serper

This is usually the one you don’t want to call frequently (cost + quotas).

Keep it hourly (or even less frequent for long-tail celebs).

So: If you must pick one to “update more frequently”, pick Wikipedia, but don’t expect it to change every 10 minutes. Use it as the fast lane input plus local signals (below).

The trick that makes it feel live even when APIs are static
Add “live dynamics” that don’t require new API calls

You already have signals that can update continuously:

user voting / approval events

your internal “rankChange” logic from snapshots

EMA smoothing state (you can “advance” it)

cached API responses (you can re-score with new weights or time decay)

So the fast lane job can do:

time decay / momentum drift: slight re-ranking based on last-known velocity

micro-updates: recompute the displayed ordering from cached data + drift

small “velocity-only” adjustments (like ±0.1–0.5% score moves)

This creates motion without lying.

How I’d implement it (simple + safe)
A) Split your score into two components

Mass (slow): your big stable score from full ingestion

Velocity (fast): short-term momentum score updated every 5–10 min

Final score = mass_score + (velocity_score * v_weight)

Where v_weight is small (e.g., 0.10–0.25).
So the board moves a bit, but doesn’t whiplash.

B) Fast lane uses:

Latest cached signals from last full ingest

Optional: Wikipedia refresh (if changed)

A decay model: velocity slowly cools unless reinforced

C) Slow lane “re-anchors”

Every hour the full ingest recalculates mass + velocity properly and overwrites drift.

UI/UX: make it feel alive and trustworthy

Do both labels:

“Live ticks every ~10 min” (fast lane)

“Full refresh about hourly” (slow lane)

And show two timestamps:

Live updated: “2m ago”

Full refresh: “47m ago”

This instantly prevents “you said it updates, but nothing changed” complaints.

One warning (so you don’t hurt trust)

If your fast lane causes visible reshuffles that later “snap back” on the hourly run, users will feel it’s fake.

That’s why:

keep fast-lane weight small,

keep drift smooth,

and don’t let fast lane make massive jumps.
