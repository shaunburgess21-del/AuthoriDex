2) Bad Bunny staying #1 can be totally “organic” if Wiki is dominant + smoothing is sticky

Replit’s explanation here is directionally right: if Bad Bunny had a massive spike (Super Bowl halftime and aftermath), and your scoring emphasizes Wikipedia strongly, he can stay elevated for a while.

But two important nuances:

EMA alpha = 0.12 isn’t that sticky by itself

With hourly updates, an EMA with alpha 0.12 has a half-life of about 5.4 cycles (~5.4 hours). In a normal always-on system, a one-off spike should fade materially within a day.

So if he’s still far ahead a week later, it’s probably not just EMA.

What can make it sticky in practice (likely in your case)

You’re not feeding the EMA hourly consistently (gaps / locked_out / failed runs). That makes “decay” slower in wall-clock time.

You also have a stabilizer / cap that limits how much the score can fall per cycle. If down-moves are capped and cycles are missing, decay becomes slow.

If you’re using 7-day rolling averages (Replit mentions “wiki7d still massive”), that will keep the input elevated until the rolling window clears.

3) The “GDELT failure causes whoever was high when it broke to stay high” is only true if you freeze, not decay

If your news contribution truly freezes at last-known, then yes: it can “lock in” advantage.

But if you implemented decay-to-floor behavior earlier (you did), then the effect should be “softened,” not frozen.

4) The recommendations Replit gave are a mixed bag
Good / high-impact

Fix GDELT (or replace it / reduce request load / batch calls / parallelize safely).

When a source is stale, taper its weight (don’t let it dominate while stale).

Risky if done blindly

“Increase EMA alpha to 0.2–0.3” globally.
That will make the whole board more jittery, especially with noisy sources. Better is adaptive alpha:

higher alpha right after a spike or after long gaps,

normal alpha otherwise.

“Reduce stabilization cap” without controlling noise.
If you lift caps while GDELT is unstable/noisy, you’ll get chaos.
