This is excellent—you’ve now got the thing that makes systems feel “professionally run”: one clean, consistent heartbeat line per job.

Why this matters so much

Before, you had to mentally stitch together:

source health logs

fallback logs

churn alerts

stabilization stats

cache hits

row counts

Now you can literally scan one line and know: “is the engine healthy?”

That’s how you stop being surprised.

How to interpret your [HEALTH SUMMARY] at a glance
✅ “All good” looks like:

rows: 100

lock: acquired

sources: OK/OK/OK

fallbacks: 0 (or small like 1–5)

rateLimitedPct: < 10–15%

churn: Top10 maybe +0/-0 to +2/-2, Top20 +0/-0 to +4/-4

⚠️ “Something is off” flags:

sources shows DEGRADED or OUTAGE

fallbacks.news or fallbacks.search jumps high (e.g., 40–100)

rateLimitedPct stays high for many runs (e.g., >25% repeatedly)

churn spikes (Top10 >4 or Top20 >8) regularly

🚨 “Emergency” flags:

rows != 100 (should now rollback, but if you ever see it, it’s a bug)

lock != acquired (means a writer collision or job overlap is happening)

avgFameIndex suddenly collapses or is near your guard threshold

Two tiny upgrades I’d still consider (optional but very helpful)
1) Add “coverage” per source (not just fallbacks)

Right now you have fallbacks counts, but it’s useful to also log:

fresh.news: 100

fresh.search: 92

fresh.wiki: 100

Because:

fallbacks might be low even when freshness is low (depending on decay logic)

coverage gate decisions are easier to understand

2) Add a “data timestamp” marker

Add the hour bucket being written, e.g.:

"hour": "2026-02-04T14:00:00Z"

This makes it obvious when you’re looking at a delayed run or re-run.

What you can do now (practical workflow)

When you check your leaderboard and something feels weird:

Open logs → search [HEALTH SUMMARY]

Look at the most recent ingest summary

If sources OK and fallbacks low, the ranking is probably legitimately reflecting your inputs (even if it’s surprising).

If news/search degraded/outage, you immediately know it’s a data-source day.
